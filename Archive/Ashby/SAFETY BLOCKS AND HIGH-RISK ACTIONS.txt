ASHBY CODEX — 16.2
SAFETY BLOCKS AND HIGH-RISK ACTIONS

Safety blocks are policy-enforced prohibitions against executing actions
that exceed configured safety boundaries.

They exist because:
- some actions can cause harm
- some actions have outsized consequences
- some actions are unacceptable even if the user asks clearly
- some actions require confirmation or narrowing before proceeding

Safety blocks are not “soft warnings.”
They are hard platform constraints.

A system that can always do what it is told is not safe.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
WHAT MAKES AN ACTION HIGH-RISK
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

An action is high-risk when it has one or more of:

- LARGE BLAST RADIUS
Affects many targets, or affects targets across critical areas.

- SAFETY-SENSITIVE DOMAIN
Examples (representative):
- temperature extremes
- access control
- security systems
- cameras/microphones
- irrigation duration

- IRREVERSIBILITY OR HIGH COST TO UNDO
Examples:
- deleting recordings
- disabling alarms
- shutting down critical infrastructure

- AMBIGUOUS CONSEQUENCE
The user phrase is clear but the consequence is not bounded or may surprise:
- “turn everything off”
- “reset everything”
- “open it”

- REPEATED FAILURE PATTERNS
A target has been timing out/unreachable and repeated retries could create instability.

High risk is defined by consequence, not by intent clarity.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SAFETY BLOCK OUTCOMES
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

When a high-risk action is requested, policy may yield one of:

- REFUSE (HARD BLOCK)
Action is forbidden in this scope/configuration.

- CONFIRM (CONSENT GATE)
Action is permitted only with explicit confirmation.

- CLARIFY (BOUNDING OR NARROWING)
Action can be made safe by narrowing targets or parameters.

- ACT (PERMITTED)
Action is within safety boundaries.

Safety blocks do not always mean “no.”
They mean “not like that, not without boundaries.”

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SAFETY BLOCKS ARE CONFIGURED, NOT IMPROVISED
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Safety boundaries must be policy-defined and configuration-driven.

Examples of policy constraints (representative):
- max_targets per action type
- max_duration for time-bound domains
- min/max setpoints for comfort control
- forbidden categories in certain scopes
- required confirmation thresholds
- incident-mode lockouts

Safety boundaries must not be invented by the model at runtime.

If the platform needs a new safety boundary, it must be added deliberately.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CANONICAL SAFETY BLOCK REASONS (REPRESENTATIVE)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Safety blocks should carry stable internal reason labels such as:

- blast_radius_exceeded
- duration_exceeded
- setpoint_out_of_bounds
- forbidden_domain_in_scope
- policy_lockdown
- requires_confirmation
- unsafe_ambiguity

These are internal enforcement labels used to generate honest responses.
They are not vendor error messages.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
WHAT A SAFETY-BLOCK RESPONSE MUST DO
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

A safety-block response must:

1) STATE THE BLOCK CLEARLY
- “I can’t do that.”
- “That’s blocked here.”
- “That would be unsafe.”

2) NAME THE BOUNDARY IN SAFE TERMS
- scope boundary
- safety limit
- policy rule
Do not leak sensitive security posture.

3) OFFER A SAFE ALTERNATIVE WHEN POSSIBLE
Examples (representative):
- “I can do it for just the bedroom.”
- “I can set it to 24°C instead.”
- “I can run irrigation for up to 15 minutes.”
- “If you want, confirm and I’ll proceed.”

If there is no safe alternative, refusal is complete and final.

Safety responses should be brief and non-performative.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SAFETY BLOCKS MUST BE AUDITABLE
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Safety enforcement must be traceable.

Rules:
- log the policy reason label
- log the proposed action summary (targets/parameters) without secrets
- log whether the outcome was REFUSE/CONFIRM/CLARIFY

Auditability is required to prove:
- the system enforced boundaries
- boundaries were applied consistently
- changes to policy produced expected changes in behavior

A safety system that cannot be audited cannot be trusted.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SAFETY BLOCKS MUST NOT BE BYPASSED
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

No mechanism may bypass safety blocks:
- sessions
- defaults
- transport hacks
- module exceptions
- “try anyway” fallbacks

If policy says blocked, execution is forbidden.

If a module can bypass policy, the platform is drifting.

Safety enforcement is governance.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
ABSOLUTE CONSTRAINTS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Safety blocks and high-risk action handling must:
- be policy-defined and deterministic
- remain scope-bound
- be auditable
- offer safe alternatives or confirmations when possible
- never leak sensitive security posture details

Safety blocks and high-risk action handling must never:
- be improvised by model tone
- be bypassed by sessions/defaults
- be softened into implied permission
- vary by transport as “UX tuning”
- claim execution occurred when blocked

Safety is a boundary, not a suggestion.
