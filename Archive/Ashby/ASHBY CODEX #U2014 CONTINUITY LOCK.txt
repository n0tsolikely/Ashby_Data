ASHBY CODEX — CONTINUITY LOCK
AUTHORSHIP, TONE, AND EXECUTION CONSTRAINTS

This document exists to preserve continuity across chats, sessions, and rebuilds
while writing the Ashby Codex.

It is binding.

Any continuation of the Codex must comply with the rules below.
Violation of these rules invalidates the Codex as a coherent work.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1) AUTHORIAL VOICE & INTENT
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The Ashby Codex is written as:
- a single, unified voice
- authoritative, not conversational
- confident, not speculative
- explicit, not implied
- disciplined, not verbose
- human-readable, not casual

The tone must be:
- calm
- deliberate
- serious
- principled
- precise

The Codex must never:
- sound like a chat transcript
- reference “earlier messages”
- reference “this conversation”
- explain why something was written
- hedge its authority

Each section must read as if written
by the same author in the same sitting,
even when produced across multiple sessions.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2) NUMBERING & STRUCTURE (NON-NEGOTIABLE)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The Table of Contents is canonical.

Before writing ANY subsection:
- verify the exact section number
- verify the exact section title
- verify the correct part

No section may be:
- skipped
- renumbered
- renamed
- merged
- split without explicit numbering (e.g., 11.1 — 1/2)

Numbering must remain consistent across the entire Codex.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3) ONE SUBSECTION = ONE DELIVERABLE
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Each subsection (e.g., 4.2, 11.1, 17.3) must be delivered as its own text document.

Rules:
- Even short subsections are standalone
- No cramming multiple subsections into one document
- No combining “related” subsections
- No collapsing content for brevity

If a subsection is long:
- split into X/Y parts (e.g., 14.3 — 1/3)
- clearly label each part
- complete all parts before moving on

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
4) ZERO TRUNCATION POLICY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Truncation is forbidden.

After writing each subsection, the author must perform a self-audit:
- confirm no ideas were compressed unnaturally
- confirm no concepts were implied instead of stated
- confirm no sentences were cut short
- confirm the section feels complete on its own

If truncation is detected:
- the section must be rewritten
- split into multiple parts if necessary
- previous output must be discarded

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
5) EXAMPLES VS INVARIANTS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The Codex distinguishes strictly between:
- invariants (law)
- examples (illustrative only)

Examples must always be framed as:
- non-exhaustive
- representative
- replaceable

The Codex must never imply that:
- listed modules are complete
- listed agents are fixed
- listed vendors are authoritative

The platform shape is invariant.
Instances are not.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
6) EXPANSION SAFETY
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Adding a new module, agent, or integration must NOT:
- invalidate the Codex
- require renumbering
- require rewriting existing sections

The Codex defines patterns, not inventories.

If a future addition requires changing the Codex:
- that change is foundational
- a Control Sync is required
- the Codex must be revised deliberately

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
7) RESPONSE DISCIPLINE DURING HANDOFF
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

When documents are uploaded for continuity handoff:
- no responses are to be generated
- no commentary is to be offered
- no questions are to be asked
- analysis only

Responses resume ONLY when the user issues
a clear instruction to proceed.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
8) RESUME PROTOCOL
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Before resuming writing after a handoff:
- review all uploaded Codex sections
- review the Table of Contents
- confirm last completed subsection
- confirm next subsection to write
- reaffirm adherence to this Continuity Lock

Only then may writing resume.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
9) PURPOSE OF THIS LOCK
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

This Continuity Lock exists so that:
- the Ashby Codex reads as a single work
- no tonal drift occurs
- no structural drift occurs
- no authority is weakened
- no accidental reinterpretation occurs

This document protects the integrity of the Codex
across time, tools, and sessions.

ASHBY CODEX — CONTINUITY LOCK ADDENDUM
SUBSECTION SPLITTING, LENGTH AWARENESS, AND RETROACTIVE CORRECTION RULES

This addendum supplements and strengthens the existing Continuity Lock.
It is binding for all future Ashby Codex writing.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1) LENGTH AWARENESS AS A FIRST-CLASS CONSTRAINT
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Every subsection in the Table of Contents must be treated as potentially large.

The author must assume by default that:
- any subsection may require multiple parts
- especially architectural, engine, world-model, or tree-instantiation sections
- especially sections that enumerate structures, directories, rules, or examples

The goal is completeness, not compression.

No subsection may be shortened, summarized, or thinned
to fit within a single response.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2) MANDATORY POST-WRITE SELF-AUDIT (RETROACTIVE ALLOWED)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

After writing any subsection, the author MUST perform a self-audit and ask:

- Did I reduce detail to fit length?
- Did I avoid elaboration because of response size?
- Did I omit examples, structure, or rules that belong here?
- Does this subsection feel artificially constrained?

If the answer to ANY is “yes”:

- The subsection is considered TRUNCATED
- The output is INVALID
- The subsection must be rewritten and split into parts

This audit may occur:
- immediately after writing
- OR retroactively, even if the subsection has already been output

Retroactive correction is REQUIRED if truncation is detected.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3) SUBSECTION PART SPLITTING RULE (CANONICAL FORMAT)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

When a subsection must be split, the numbering MUST follow this exact pattern:

- X.Y — 1/2
- X.Y — 2/2

or, if necessary:

- X.Y — 1/3
- X.Y — 2/3
- X.Y — 3/3

Rules:
- The subsection number (X.Y) DOES NOT change
- Parts are ordered and explicit
- All parts must be completed before moving to the next subsection
- Earlier truncated outputs must be explicitly discarded

Example (illustrative only):
- 6.2 — 1/3
- 6.2 — 2/3
- 6.2 — 3/3

The author must clearly indicate that the subsection is split
and that all parts together form the complete subsection.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
4) PREFERENCE FOR OVER-SPLITTING
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

When in doubt, the author MUST prefer:
- splitting too much
- rather than splitting too little

It is acceptable for:
- a subsection to occupy an entire response
- a subsection to be split even if it could technically fit
- short subsections to remain standalone

It is NOT acceptable for:
- a subsection to feel rushed
- a subsection to lack depth
- a subsection to imply content instead of stating it

The user will manually recombine parts later.
Clean separation is preferred over compactness.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
6) ZERO PENALTY FOR CORRECTION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Detecting truncation and correcting it is not a failure.
It is a requirement.

The author must:
- flag truncation explicitly

- reissue the subsection correctly split

This behavior is expected and correct.
